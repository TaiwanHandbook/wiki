Taiwan's Journey

In his autobiography, Chen Shui-bian recalls that his mother, a poor farming tenant, would put him in a hole near the fields she worked, as a substitute for a playpen. Over the next 50 years, Chen managed to hoist himself from the hole in the ground to the top university of the country, to being the attorneys of leading pro-democracy politicians and activists, eventually winning the 2000 presidential election, becoming the first president of Taiwan from the Democratic Progressive Party, and signalling the end of the Kuomintang's hegemony.

In parallel, Taiwan, formally the Republic of China, was undergoing equivalent developments, both economically and politically. From an increasingly isolated, extremely repressive and authoritarian island nation, plagued by hiperinflation in the early 1950s, Taiwan has experienced some of the fastest economic growth and democratisation in history. It managed to transform itself into the most developed democracy of the region, and an essential cog of the international markets.
